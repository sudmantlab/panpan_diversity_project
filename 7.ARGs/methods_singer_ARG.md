# Methods — SINGER ARG analyses - Long version



**Ancestral recombination graph analyses**

To identify candidate targets of population-specific positive selection, we reconstructed the local genealogical history of chimpanzees, bonobos and humans using Bayesian inference of ancestral recombination graphs (ARGs) with SINGER, broadly following the analytical framework of Deng et al.

*VCF preparation.* Per-haplotype SNV calls from long-read primary assemblies were merged with `bcftools merge` and converted into single-column phased diploid genotypes (`0|0`, `1|0`, `0|1`, `1|1`) with a custom script (`haplo2diplo.py`); positions missing on both haplotypes were imputed as homozygous reference and any other inconsistencies were set to missing. Per-sample diploid VCFs were merged per species, sorted, indexed, and restricted to bi-allelic SNVs (`bcftools view --types snps --min-alleles 2 --max-alleles 2`). Chimpanzees were mapped to mPanTro3, bonobos to mPanPan1, and humans (HPRC + HGSVC; 1000 Genomes 579-sample panel) to T2T-CHM13v2.0.

*Genome partitioning.* For each species, the autosomal genome was split into non-overlapping 5 Mb windows after subtracting curated centromere coordinates (`bedtools subtract -a chromosomes_sorted.bed -b centromeres_sorted.bed | bedtools makewindows -w 5000000`); centromeres were taken from the GenomeFeatures BED files for mPanTro3 v0.9, mPanPan1 v1.0 and T2T-CHM13 v1.1. Each VCF was split into the resulting chunks with `bcftools view -r`.

*SINGER inference.* SINGER (`singer_master`) was run on each 5 Mb chunk with μ = 1.25 × 10<sup>-8</sup> per bp per generation and species-specific effective population sizes (N<sub>e</sub> = 2 × 10<sup>4</sup> for humans; N<sub>e</sub> = 4 × 10<sup>4</sup> for chimpanzees and bonobos), using SINGER's default MCMC settings (1,000 burn-in iterations, thinning 20, 100 posterior samples per chunk).

*Per-chromosome ARG merging.* Posterior chunks belonging to the same chromosome and the same MCMC sample index were stitched into a single chromosome-wide tree sequence with a custom script (`merge_ARGs.py`) built on `tskit.TableCollection`: node times were preserved across chunks (with a +1 × 10<sup>-7</sup> tie-breaker enforced for monotonicity), edge intervals were shifted by the chunk's genomic start coordinate, and mutation positions were lifted to absolute chromosome coordinates. This yielded 100 posterior chromosome-wide tree sequences per chromosome, written as `tskit` `.trees` files.

*Per-window coalescence statistics.* Each posterior tree sequence was scanned in non-overlapping 1 kb windows (`np.arange(0, chrom_length+1000, 1000)`) using custom `tskit`-based scripts (`windowed_tmrca_stats.py`, `windowed_tmrca_stats_byPOP.py`) to compute three statistics:

- **Average TMRCA**: for each marginal tree, the mean of `tree.time(root)` was weighted by the tree's intersection length with the window; per-window TMRCA was then averaged across the 100 posterior ARG samples.
- **T<sub>pooled</sub>**: the average pairwise coalescence time across all sampled haplotypes pooled, computed in branch mode as `ts.diversity(sample_sets=[all_samples], windows=windows, mode="branch") / 2` and averaged across the 100 posterior ARGs.
- **T<sub>within</sub>**: the average pairwise coalescence time within each population, computed identically but with `sample_sets` set to the haplotype indices of each population (Central, Eastern and Western chimpanzees following `populations.csv`; superpopulations for humans; bonobos analysed as a single panel because of limited sample size).

The T<sub>pooled</sub>/T<sub>within</sub> ratio was computed per 1 kb window per population by dividing the posterior-averaged T<sub>pooled</sub> by the posterior-averaged T<sub>within</sub> of that population. Branch-mode `diversity` divided by two recovers the expected pairwise coalescence time, equivalent to the T<sub>pooled</sub> and T<sub>within</sub> estimators of Deng et al. Each 1 kb window was annotated with overlapping genes from the corresponding T2T-Primates assembly (`mPanTro3_gene.bed.gz`, `mPanPan1_gene.bed.gz`, `ht2t_gene.bed.gz`) using `bedtools map`. Coalescence times were converted from generations to millions of years assuming species-specific generation times of 25 years for chimpanzees and bonobos and 28 years for humans.

*Two-stage filtering of genome-wide TMRCA windows.* All downstream analyses were performed on a filtered set of windows obtained as follows.

(1) **Coordinate-level filtering with `bedtools`** was applied to the concatenated, gene-annotated genome-wide table:
- For chimpanzees, windows overlapping any region of the centromere/satellite track were removed (`bedtools intersect -v` against `mPanTro3_CenSat.bed`), and the remainder was restricted to windows overlapping the short-read accessibility mask `mPanTro3_SR_mask.bed` (`bedtools intersect -u`).
- For bonobos, the same two operations were applied with `mPanPan1_CenSat.bed` and `mPanPan1_SR_mask.bed`.
- For humans, centromere/satellite tracks are part of the GenomeFeatures annotation and were therefore handled at the next step; only the SR-accessibility intersection (`ht2t_SR_mask.bed`, `bedtools intersect -u`) was applied at this stage.

(2) **Quantitative overlap filtering in R** was then applied to the resulting tables. For every 1 kb window we computed the fractional bp overlap (`valr::bed_intersect`, divided by the 1 kb window size) with each of the following species-specific tracks: tandem-repeat catalogue, centromere/satellite track, segmental-duplication track, RepeatMasker, short-read accessibility mask, and a combined `feature_annotation` string built from the GenomeFeatures, CenSat, RepeatMasker, TR and SEDEF tracks. Windows were then filtered as follows (sex chromosomes, unitig/unplaced contigs and hybrid individuals were excluded throughout):

- **Chimpanzees**: `censat_overlap_percent == 0`, `tr_overlap_percent ≤ 5`, `SRA_overlap_percent > 20`, and `feature_annotation` not matching any of "Satellite", "Low_complexity", "Cen", "CENSAT" or "Gap".
- **Bonobos**: `censat_overlap_percent == 0`, `tr_overlap_percent ≤ 5`, and `feature_annotation` not matching "Satellite", "Low_complexity" or "Cen".
- **Humans**: `tr_overlap_percent ≤ 5`, `SRA_overlap_percent > 20`, and `feature_annotation` not matching "Satellite", "Low_complexity", "Cen", "CEN", "SAT", "rDNA" or "Gap".

These thresholds are deliberately species-specific because the underlying reference assemblies differ in completeness, in the structure of pericentromeric regions, and in the availability of a short-read accessibility mask.

*Outlier identification.* On the filtered, gene-annotated tables, two complementary outlier criteria were applied. Windows with T<sub>pooled</sub> or T<sub>within</sub> older than the human–chimpanzee split (> 6 Mya) cannot be explained by drift alone and were flagged as candidates for long-term balancing selection or trans-species polymorphism. Population-specific selective sweeps, by contrast, reduce local within-population diversity and therefore inflate T<sub>pooled</sub>/T<sub>within</sub>; following Deng et al., we flagged windows in which this ratio exceeded the 99.99th percentile of its per-population genome-wide distribution (`PERCENTILE_THRESHOLD = 0.9999`), where they appear as peaks along the genome. Per-gene outlier tables were produced by population (Figure 4E–K, Supplementary Tables Sx–Sz).

*Visualisation and GO enrichment.* All windowed statistics were visualised in R with `ggplot2` as chromosome-faceted Manhattan plots, with each population's 99.99th-percentile outlier threshold drawn as a horizontal dashed line; zoom-in panels around candidate loci were re-plotted from the full *unfiltered* per-window table (`coalescence_df_with_features`, before the stage-2 quantitative overlap filters — no ≤ 5 % tandem-repeat cap and no > 20 % short-read accessibility-mask requirement) to expose the local TMRCA trajectory across all 1 kb windows. Gene-Ontology enrichment for the cross-species shared outliers with TMRCA predating the human–chimpanzee split was tested in ShinyGO v0.82 (Ge et al., 2020) with pathway sizes of 15–1,000 genes, retaining pathways with FDR-corrected p < 0.01 (Figure 4E–K, Supplementary Tables Sx–Sz).

*Gene-name harmonisation across species.* Because chimpanzee and bonobo gene annotations contain many species-specific names for one-to-one human orthologues (e.g. chimpanzee `PATR-A`, `PATR-B`, `PATR-DPB1` → human `HLA-A`, `HLA-B`, `HLA-DPB1`) as well as uncharacterised `LOC*` entries whose NCBI descriptions correspond to known human genes (e.g. `LOC471948` → `HLA-A`), gene names were first harmonised to their human orthologue symbols using a curated mapping table (`apply_gene_rename.py`); mappings were derived from NCBI Gene look-ups of the descriptive annotation text and cross-checked against a manually curated reference list. The renamed symbols (`genes_renamed`) were then used as the cross-species join key in all overlap analyses below.

*Cross-species and cross-population overlap of selection candidates.* To assess overlap of selection candidates across species and populations, the per-population outlier gene lists (Central, Eastern and Western chimpanzees; African, East Asian, European, South Asian and American humans; bonobos as a single cohort) were combined by `genes_renamed` with two complementary R scripts (`make_wide_tables.R` and `make_species_only_tables.R`). Each gene was annotated with the set of species in which it was an outlier (`species_sharing` ∈ {all three, bonobo+chimp, bonobo+human, chimp+human, bonobo only, chimp only, human only}) and, for chimpanzees and humans, the set of populations within each species in which it reached outlier status (`chimp_pop_sharing`, `human_pop_sharing`). This yielded a species+population-resolved overlap summary (Supplementary Table Sx) — preserving the per-population resolution at which outliers were originally called — and a species-only collapsed version (Supplementary Table Sy) for species-level comparisons.
